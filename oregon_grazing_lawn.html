Ungulate Maintained Grazing Lawns in a Temperate Coastal Rainforest

Samuel T. Woodrich, William J. Ripple, Robert L. Beschta

ABSTRACT: Grazing lawns have not previously been documented in temperate coastal rainforests, but their formation may result from concentrated herbivory by native ungulates. We searched for grazing lawn habitats in forests along the central Oregon coast and identified their distinguishing structural and compositional features. We conducted exclosure experiments to assess the effect continual maintenance by native ungulates has on grazing lawns, and the ecological effects associated with grazing lawn presence in the ecosystem. We found grazing lawns within our study area frequented and maintained by Roosevelt elk (Cervus canadensis roosevelti) and black-tailed deer (Odocoileus hemionus columbianus). These lawns demonstrated three consistent characteristics: (1) shrub heights were predominantly at or below the general upper level of ungulate browsing, (2) fruit production was generally absent from fruit-producing shrubs, and (3) plants with browsing resistant traits were not frequently browsed. Furthermore, frequent herbivory by native ungulates has contributed to a recruitment gap in native shrubs, while less palatable plants persist. Grazing lawns were also associated with increased cover of alien plants in the herbaceous layer, suggesting that repeated herbivory gives these species a competitive advantage. When ungulate herbivory was eliminated by an exclosure, vegetation composition and structure of grazing lawns began to return toward those typical of the surrounding forest. Although alien plants were dominant in grazing lawns, they were absent inside long-term ungulate exclosures, indicating the cessation of herbivory can shift plant structure and composition towards conditions of the surrounding forest. Our results indicate frequent native ungulate herbivory can maintain grazing lawns in a rainforest ecosystem, where they had not previously been identified.
