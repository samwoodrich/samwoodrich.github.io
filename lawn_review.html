A Systematic Review of Grazing Lawn Ecology

Samuel T. Woodrich & William J. Ripple

ABSTRACT: Although several reviews of grazing lawn ecology exist, a systematic synthesis of where grazing lawns occur, which animals maintain them, and how they are defined has not been conducted. We conducted a systematic review of all grazing lawn publications made since 1984 when the first paper on the subject was published. Following the preferred reporting items for systematic reviews and meta-analyses (PRISMA) guidelines for ecology and evolutionary biology, we recorded the following for each selected paper: (1) the research location site name, protection status, ecoregion, biome, and continent, (2) each animal species present in the study area, and (3) the grazing lawn definition provided. Using these data, we examined whether predators and predation effects are lacking in grazing lawn research, identified geographic and taxonomic patterns in grazing lawn studies, and evaluated how grazing lawn definitions have changed over time. Predators were present in most study systems but were incorporated into relatively few study designs, suggesting their influence on grazing lawn dynamics may be underappreciated. Additionally, grazing lawn research has predominantly taken place within protected areas, the protection of which may influence the formation and maintenance of grazing lawns. Furthermore, recently identified grazing lawn maintaining herbivores are gregarious by nature, reaffirming gregariousness as an influential factor in a species ability to create and maintain grazing lawns. Existing grazing lawn definitions have omitted stoloniferous (lawn) grasses as a characteristic of lawn vegetation, and instead described bunch grasses, sedges, and forbs as possible plant types in grazing lawns. As such, we provide a modern grazing lawn definition that incorporates the characteristics of new ecoregions and animal species capable of maintaining them, defined as “a patch with increased nutritional value and short statured vegetation maintained by grazing herbivores.” Our review suggests that many traditional assumptions about grazing lawns, largely based on African systems, may need revision as research expands to new ecosystems and herbivore assemblages around the world.
